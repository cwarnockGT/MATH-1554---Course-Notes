Example 5.17.

Let
🔗
\begin{equation*} M=\begin{bmatrix} a \amp b\\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Express the characteristic equation of \(M\) in terms of \(\det(M)\text{.}\) What is the equation when \(M\) is singular?
🔗
Solution.
Note that
🔗
\(\begin{eqnarray*} \det(M - \lambda I) \amp = \amp \begin{vmatrix} a-\lambda \amp b \\ c \amp d - \lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - a\lambda - d\lambda + ad - bc \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc). \end{eqnarray*}\)
We see that the constant term of the characteristic polynomial is \(ad - bc = \det(M)\text{.}\) Furthermore, the linear coefficient is \(-(a + d) = -\operatorname{tr}(M)\text{,}\) where \(\operatorname{tr}(M)\) is the trace of \(M\) and is the sum of its diagonal elements. When \(M\) is singular, we have \(\det(M) = 0\text{,}\) so the characteristic polynomial is \(\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda\text{,}\) which has a factor of \(\lambda\text{.}\) Therefore, we see that \(\lambda_1 = 0\) is an eigenvalue of \(M\text{.}\)
🔗
Suppose \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) are the eigenvalues of \(M\text{.}\) Then, we have that
\begin{equation*} \lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(M)\lambda + \det(M) = (\lambda - \lambda_1)(\lambda - \lambda_2) = \lambda^2 - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)\lambda + \lambda_1\lambda_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
It follows that \(\operatorname{tr}(M)\) is the sum of the eigenvalues of \(M\) and \(\det(M)\) is the product of the eigenvalues of \(M\text{.}\) This is no coincidence and is true for all \(n\times n\) matrices.
