Theorem 7.15. Orthogonality of Eigenspaces.

If \(A\) is symmetric and \(\vec v_1,\vec v_2\) are eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues, then \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are orthogonal.
🔗
More generally, eigenspaces associated with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal subspaces.
🔗
Solution.
Suppose \(A^T = A\) and suppose \(\vec v_1, \vec v_2\) are eigenvectors of \(A\) corresponding to distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\text{,}\) respectively. Thus, \(A\vec v_1 = \lambda_1 \vec v_1\) and \(A\vec v_2 = \lambda_2\vec v_2\text{.}\)
🔗
Our goal is to show that \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are orthogonal. Therefore, we want to show that \(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2 = 0\text{.}\) Note the following:
\begin{align*} \lambda_1(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2) \amp = (\lambda_1\vec v_1)\cdot \vec v_2 \amp \quad \text{Associativity of the dot product}\\ \amp = (A\vec v_1) \cdot \vec v_2 \amp \quad \text{Definition of eigenvector}\\ \amp = (A\vec v_1)^T\vec v_2 \amp \quad \text{Definition of dot product}\\ \amp = \vec v_1^TA^T\vec v_2 \amp \quad \text{Distributing transpose}\\ \amp = \vec v_1^TA\vec v_2 \amp \quad \text{A is symmetric}\\ \amp = \vec v_1 \cdot (A\vec v_2) \amp \quad \text{Definition of dot product}\\ \amp = \vec v_1 \cdot (\lambda_2 \vec v_2) \amp \quad \text{Definition of eigenvector}\\ \amp = \lambda_2(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2) \amp \quad \text{Associativity of the dot product} \end{align*}
Thus, we have that
\begin{equation*} \lambda_1(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2) = \lambda_2(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Subtracting the right side expression from both sides yields
\begin{equation*} (\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)(\vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2) = 0 \end{equation*}
Since \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\text{,}\) we have that \(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 \neq 0\text{.}\) Therefore, it must be the case that
\begin{equation*} \vec v_1 \cdot \vec v_2 = 0 \end{equation*}
Hence, \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are orthogonal.
