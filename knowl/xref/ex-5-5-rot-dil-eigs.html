Example 5.52.

The matrix that rotates vectors by \(\phi=\pi/4\) about the origin and then scales by \(r=\sqrt{2}\) is
🔗
\begin{equation*} A= \begin{bmatrix} r \amp 0\\ 0 \amp r \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\phi \amp -\sin\phi\\ \sin\phi \amp \cos\phi \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1\\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Find the eigenvalues of \(A\) and one eigenvector for each eigenvalue.
🔗
Solution.
Note that
\begin{equation*} \det(A - \lambda I) = \begin{vmatrix} 1-\lambda \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 1 - \lambda\end{vmatrix} = (1-\lambda)^2 + 1 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 2\text{.} \end{equation*}
Using the quadratic formula, we find the eigenvalues of \(A\) are
\begin{equation*} \lambda = \dfrac{2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 4(1)(2)}}{2} = \dfrac{2 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} = \dfrac{2 \pm 2i}{2} = 1 \pm i\text{.} \end{equation*}
Let \(\lambda_1 = 1+i\) and \(\lambda_2 = 1-i\text{.}\) Note that these are conjuage pairs as suggested by Theorem 5.50.
🔗
Note that \(\lambda_1\) can be written in polar form as
\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 = \|\lambda_1\|e^{i\operatorname{arctan}\left(\frac{1}{1}\right)} = \sqrt{2}e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We see that \(r = \|\lambda_1\| = \sqrt{2}\) gives the scaling factor of \(A\) and \(\phi = \frac{\pi}{4}\) gives the rotation factor of \(A\text{.}\)
🔗
Now, let’s find at least one eigenvector for each eigenvalue. For \(\lambda_1\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} A - \lambda_1 I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 + i \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 + i \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -i \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp -i \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
At first glance, \(A - \lambda_1 I\) might not appear to have a free variable. Hopefully it does, or else we made an error somewhere. Note that we can still use row operations on \(A\) even though it contains complex numbers. This, hopefully, will expose the dependency of the columns. Note that
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -i \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp -i \end{bmatrix} \overset{\text{Swap $R_1$ and $R_2$}}{\LARGE \sim} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -i \\ -i \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \overset{R_2 + iR_1 \rightarrow R_2}{\LARGE \sim} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -i \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Now, we see that
\begin{equation*} \vec v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \in \Nul(A - \lambda_1 I)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Let’s check that \(A\vec v_1 = \lambda_1 \vec v_1\text{.}\) Note that
\begin{equation*} A\vec v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} i - 1 \\ i + 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \vec v_1 = (1 + i)\begin{bmatrix} i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} i + i^2 \\ 1 + i \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} i - 1 \\ i + 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
so \(A\vec v_1 = \lambda_1 \vec v_1\text{.}\)
🔗
By Theorem 5.50, we have that
\begin{equation*} \vec v_2 = \overline{\vec v_1} = \begin{bmatrix} \overline{i} \\ \overline{1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is an eigenvector for \(\lambda_2 = \overline{\lambda_1}\text{.}\)
