Example 6.52.

Consider the system \(A\vec x = \vec b\) for
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \vec b = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
(a)
Is the system consistent?
🔗
Solution.
Note that
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp \rvert \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 11 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 11 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp \frac{21}{2}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Therefore, there is no solution and the system is inconsistent.
🔗
🔗
🔗
(b)
Find the vector \(\widehat b = \proj_{\Col(A)}\vec b\text{.}\)
🔗
Solution.
In order to project onto \(\Col(A)\text{,}\) we need an orthogonal basis of \(\Col(A)\text{.}\) Since the columns of \(A\) are not orthogonal, we start by using the Gram-Schmidt Process. If we assign the columns of \(A\) as \(\vec x_1\) and \(\vec x_2\text{,}\) respectively, then we have that
\begin{align*} \vec v_1 \amp = \vec x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \vec v_2 \amp = \vec x_2 - \proj_{\vec v_1}\vec x_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} - \dfrac{1}{17}\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \dfrac{1}{17}\begin{bmatrix}-4 \\ 34 \\ 16\end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
We will use \(\vec v_2' = 17\vec v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ 34 \\ 16 \end{bmatrix}\) in place of \(\vec v_2\text{.}\) So, \(\left\{\vec v_1, \vec v_2'\right\}\) is an orthogonal basis for \(\Col(A).\text{.}\) Therefore, we have that
\begin{align*} \widehat b \amp = \proj_{\Col(A)}\vec b\\ \amp = \proj_{\vec v_1}\vec b + \proj_{\vec v_2'}\vec b\\ \amp = \dfrac{19}{17}\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \dfrac{2}{17}\begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ 34 \\ 16 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \dfrac{1}{17}\begin{bmatrix} 76 - 8 \\ 0 + 68 \\ 19 + 32 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
So, \(\widehat b = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) is the vector in \(\Col(A)\) which is as close as possible to \(\vec b\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗
🔗
(c)
Find the least squares solution to \(A\vec x = \vec b\text{.}\)
🔗
Solution.
We will solve the equation \(A\vec x = \widehat b\text{.}\) Note that
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp \rvert \amp 4 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \rvert \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \rvert \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus, the least-squares solution is \(\widehat x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Notice the error from our least-squares solution is
\begin{equation*} \|\vec b - A\widehat x\| = \|\vec b - \widehat b\| = \left\|\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\right\| = \sqrt{4 + 16 + 64} = \sqrt{84}. \end{equation*}
