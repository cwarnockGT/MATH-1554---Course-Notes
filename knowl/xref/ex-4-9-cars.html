Example 4.7.

A rental company has three locations A, B, and C. Cars may be returned to any location. The table gives the weekly rental/return pattern:
There are 18 cars at each location today.
🔗
(a)
Construct the stochastic matrix \(P\text{.}\) Is \(P \) regular?
🔗
Solution.
We can build the following table describing how the cars move between the three rental companies. It follows that
\begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.6 \amp 0.3 \\ 0 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(P\) has at least one entry which is 0, it is not clear whether \(P\) is regular or not. By Definition 4.5, in order to show \(P\) is regular, we need to show that some power of \(P\) has no entries which are 0. Note that
\begin{equation*} P^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.6 \amp 0.3 \\ 0 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.5 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.2 \amp 0.6 \amp 0.3 \\ 0 \amp 0.3 \amp 0.5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.66 \amp 0.2 \amp 0.29 \\ 0.28 \amp 0.47 \amp 0.37 \\ 0.06 \amp 0.33 \amp 0.34 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Since every entry of \(P^2\) is positive, we have that \(P\) is regular. Therefore, Theorem 4.6 guarantees that \(P\) has a unique steady-state vector.
🔗
🔗
🔗
(b)
Determine what happens to the distribution of cars after a long time.
🔗
Solution.
Again, we need to find a probability vector in \(\Nul(P - I).\) Note that
🔗
\(\begin{eqnarray*} P - I \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} -0.2 \amp 0.1 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.2 \amp -0.4 \amp 0.3 \\ 0 \amp 0.3 \amp -0.5 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp \sim \amp \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp -4 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 3 \amp -5 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp \sim \amp \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp -3 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 3 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp \sim \amp \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp -3 \amp 5 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\end{eqnarray*}\)
🔗
Again, we have that \(P - I\) only has a single free variable, so \(\dim(\Nul(P - I)) = 1\) which means that \(P\) will have a unique steady-state vector. We start by finding some nonzero vector in \(\Nul(P - I)\text{.}\) Since \(x_3\) is a free variable, we set \(x_3 = t \in\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then the second row gives
\begin{equation*} -3x_2 + 5x_3 = 0 \quad \implies \quad -3x_2 = -5t \quad \implies \quad x_2 = \frac{5}{3}t\text{.} \end{equation*}
The first row gives
\begin{equation*} -2x_1 + x_2 + 2x_3 = 0 \ \implies \ x_1 = -\frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{5}{3}t - 2t\right) \ \implies \ x_1 = \frac{11}{6}t\text{.} \end{equation*}
Letting \(t = 6\text{,}\) we see that
\begin{equation*} \vec v = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{11}{6}t \\ \frac{5}{3}t \\ t \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{11}{6}(6) \\ \frac{5}{3}(6) \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 10 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \in \Nul(P - I)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since the entries of \(\vec v\) add to 27, we have that
\begin{equation*} \vec q = \frac{1}{27}\vec v = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{11}{27} \\ \frac{10}{27} \\ \frac{6}{27} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is the unique steady-state vector of \(P\text{.}\) It follows that the limit vector \(\vec \ell\) is an appropriate multiple of \(\vec q\text{.}\) Since we started with a total of \(3(18) = 54\) cars, we have that
\begin{equation*} \vec \ell = 54\vec q = \begin{bmatrix} 22 \\ 20 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Therefore, after a long time, Location A will have 22 cars, Location B will have 20 cars, and Location C will have 12 cars.
