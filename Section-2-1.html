    Definition 2.1. Zero Matrix.

    A zero matrix is any matrix whose every entry is zero.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 0_{2\times 3}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 0_{2\times 1}= \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    Definition 2.2. Identity Matrix.

    The \(n\times n\) identity matrix has ones on the main diagonal and zeros elsewhere.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} I_2= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad I_3= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    Note: Any matrix with dimensions \(n\times n\) is square. Zero matrices need not be square; identity matrices must be square.

    Suppose \(A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\text{,}\) and \(a_{i,j}\) is the element of \(A\) in row \(i\) and column \(j\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    1. If \(A\) and \(B\) are \(m\times n\) matrices, then the elements of \(A+B\) are \(a_{i,j}+b_{i,j}\text{.}\)

      ðŸ”—
    2. If \(c\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then the elements of \(cA\) are \(c\,a_{i,j}\text{.}\)

    Definition 2.4. Matrix Product via Columns.

    Let \(A\) be an \(m\times n\) matrix and \(B\) be an \(n\times p\) matrix. The product \(AB\) is an \(m\times p\) matrix, given by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} AB = A \begin{bmatrix} \vec b_1 \amp \cdots \amp \vec b_p \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A\vec b_1 \amp \cdots \amp A\vec b_p \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Note: The dimensions of \(A\) and \(B\) determine whether \(AB\) is defined, and what its dimensions will be.

    If \(A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) has rows \(\vec a_i\) and \(B\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}\) has columns \(\vec b_j\text{,}\) then each element of \(C=AB\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} c_{ij}=\vec a_i\cdot \vec b_j. \end{equation*}

    Example 2.6.

    Compute the following using the row-column method.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} C=AB= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0\\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \amp 1\\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Note that
    ðŸ”—
    \(\begin{eqnarray*} AB \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 2 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot 4 \amp 2 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 5 \amp 2 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 6 \\ 1 \cdot 3 + (-1) \cdot 4 \amp 1 \cdot 0 + (-1) \cdot 5 \amp 1 \cdot 1 + (-1) \cdot 6 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp 0 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp -5 \amp -5 \end{bmatrix}\end{eqnarray*}\)
    Note that this method is faster than using the original definition of matrix multiplication given in DefinitionÂ 2.4. For example, to compute the first column of \(AB \) using the original definition, we would compute the following:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}2 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = 3\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} + 4 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 2.7. Properties and Warnings.

    Let \(A,B,C\) be matrices of sizes needed for the products to be defined, and suppose \(A\) is \(m\times n\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    1. Associative: \((AB)C = A(BC)\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Left distributive: \(A(B+C)=AB+AC\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Right distributive: \((A+B)C = AC + BC\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    4. Identity: \(I_m A = A I_n\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    Warnings:
    ðŸ”—
    1. Non-commutative: In general, \(AB\neq BA\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Non-cancellation: \(AB=AC\) does not imply \(B=C\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Zero divisors: \(AB=0\) does not imply \(A=0\) or \(B=0\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The associative property is \((AB)C = A(BC)\text{.}\) If \(C=\vec x\text{,}\) then:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} (AB)\vec x = A(B\vec x). \end{equation*}
    Schematically:
    ðŸ”—
    
    Diagram showing two ways of getting from \(\vec x\) to \(AB\vec x\text{,}\) either by \(\vec x \mapsto B\vec x \mapsto AB\vec x\) or by direct multiplication by \(\vec x \mapsto AB\vec x\text{.}\)
    The product \(AB\vec x\) can be obtained either by multiplying by \(AB\text{,}\) or by multiplying by \(B\) and then by \(A\text{.}\) This corresponds to composition of linear transformations.

    Example 2.8.

    Let
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Give an example of a \(2\times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB\neq BA\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Honestly, pretty much any matrix you choose at random will work. For example, let \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\) and note that
    \begin{equation*} AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 3 \amp 1 \cdot 2 + 1 \cdot 4 \\ 0 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 3 \amp 0 \cdot 2 + 0 \cdot 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 6 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    while
    \begin{equation*} BA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 0 \amp 1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 0 \\ 3 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 0 \amp 3 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 3 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(AB \neq BA \text{.}\)

    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad C= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Which of these operations are defined, and what are the dimensions of the result?
    ðŸ”—
    (a)
    \(A + 3C\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Note that \(A \) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix while \(3C\) is a \(3 \times 3\) matrix. Therefore, the sum \(A + 3C\) is not defined.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    (b)
    \(C(AB)^T\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    First, we can use Property (d) from TheoremÂ 2.11 to write \(C(AB)^T = CB^TA^T\text{.}\) Since \(B \) is a \(2 \times 3\) matrix, we have that \(B^T \) is a \(3 \times 2\) matrix. Since \(A \) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, we have that \(A^T \) is also a \(2 \times 2\) matrix. Thus, the product \(CB^TA^T\) is the product of a \(3 \times 3\) matrix with a \(3 \times 2 \) matrix with a \(2 \times 2\) matrix. Since all inner dimensions match, this product is defined.

    \(A + ABCB^T\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Note that \(A \) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, \(B \) is a \(2 \times 3\) matrix, \(C \) is a \(3 \times 3\) matrix, and \(B^T\) is a \(3 \times 2\) matrix.     Example 2.15.

    True or False. If false, explain briefly.
    ðŸ”—
    (a)
    Let \(n \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then for any \(n \times n\) matrix \(\text{,}\) we have \((I_n + A)(I_n - A) = I_n - A^2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Recall from TheoremÂ 2.7 that the Distributive Property applies to matrix mutliplication and addition. Therefore, we start with the distributive property to simply the product.
    \begin{equation*} (I_n + A)(I_n - A) = I_nI_n + I_n(-A) + AI_n + A(-A) \end{equation*}
    By the multiplicative property of the identity matrix, we have that \(I_nI_n = I_n\) and \(I_n(-A) = -A\) and \(AI_n = A\text{.}\) Recall from our properties of matrix multipliation that we are allowed to commute scalars with matrix multiplication, just not matrices with matrix multiplication. Therefore, we can factor out the \(-1\) and rewrite \(A(-A) = -AA = -A^2\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} (I_n + A)(I_n - A) = I_n - A + A - A^2 = I_n - A^2 \end{equation*}
    since \(-A + A\) is the zero matrix, which doesnâ€™t change anything when you add it to a term. Therefore, we see that this statement is TRUE.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    (b)
    Let \(n \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For any \(n \times n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) we have \((A+B)^2 = A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Proceeding in a similar manner to the problem above, we see that
    \begin{equation*} (A+B)^2 = (A+B)(A+B) = AA + AB + BA + BB\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can rewrite \(AA = A^2\) and \(BB = B^2\text{.}\) Also, since matrix addition is commutative, we can move the terms \(AB\) and \(BA\) to the end. This leaves us with
    \begin{equation*} (A+B)^2 = A^2 + B^2 + AB + BA\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that if \(AB = BA\text{,}\) then we could combine the middle terms into \(2AB\text{.}\) However, from the warning in TheoremÂ 2.7, we see that matrix multiplication is often not commutative. Therefore, we cannot replace \(AB + BA\) with \(2AB\text{.}\) This leads us to believe the statement is false.
    ðŸ”—
    We can prove it is false by giving a counterexample. Probably, pretty much anything we try will work since matrix commutation is very rare. Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Note that
    ðŸ”—
    \(\begin{eqnarray*}(A+B)^2 = \left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\right)^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}^2 \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 50 \amp 50 \\ 50 \amp 50 \end{bmatrix};\end{eqnarray*}\)
    ðŸ”—
    however,
    ðŸ”—
    \(\begin{eqnarray*}A^2 + B^2 + 2AB \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}^2 + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}^2 + 2\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 2 \\ 3\amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 4\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} + 2\begin{bmatrix} 8 \amp 5 \\ 20 \amp 13 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 7 \amp 10 \\ 15 \amp 22 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 22 \amp 15 \\ 10 \amp 7 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 16 \amp 10 \\ 40 \amp 26 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 45 \amp 35 \\ 65 \amp 55 \end{bmatrix}\end{eqnarray*}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Therefore, since \((A+B)^2 \neq A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\) for these particular matrices, the statement must be FALSE.
