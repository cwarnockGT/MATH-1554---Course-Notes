    Section 5.2 The Characteristic Equation

    Handout 5.2 The Characteristic Equation
    

    Objectives: Topics

    • The characteristic polynomial of a matrix
      🔗
    • Algebraic and geometric multiplicity of eigenvalues
      🔗
    🔗

    Objectives: Goals

    • Construct the characteristic polynomial of a matrix and use it to identify eigenvalues and their multiplicities.
      🔗
    • Characterize the long-term behaviour of dynamical systems using eigenvalue decompositions.
      🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Note 5.14.

    Similar matrices are introduced in Section 5.2, but are not used until Section 5.3. We will wait to introduce them in Section 5.3.
    🔗
     
    🔗
    Recall:
    🔗
    \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) if and only if \((A-\lambda I)\) is not invertible.
    🔗
    Therefore, to calculate the eigenvalues of \(A\text{,}\) we can need to find all \(\lambda\) so that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \det(A-\lambda I)=0. \end{equation*}

    Definition 5.15.

    The quantity \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) is the characteristic polynomial of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The equation \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\) is the characteristic equation of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The roots of the characteristic polynomial are the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Example 5.16.

    Find the characteristic polynomial of
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 2\\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Then determine the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that
    🔗
    \(\begin{eqnarray*} \det(A - \lambda I) \amp = \amp \begin{vmatrix} 5-\lambda \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 - \lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp (5-\lambda)(1-\lambda) - 2(2) \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 5 - 4 \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 1. \end{eqnarray*}\)
    Therefore, the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is \(\lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 1\text{.}\) We know from Definition 5.15 that the roots of this polynomial are the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) Since the polynomial doesn’t factor nicely, we use the quadratic formula to find the eigenvalues:
    \begin{equation*} \lambda = \dfrac{-(-6) \pm \sqrt{(-6)^2 - 4(1)(1)}}{2(1)} = \dfrac{6 \pm \sqrt{32}}{2} = 3 \pm 2\sqrt{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(\lambda_1 = 3 + 2\sqrt{2}\) and \(\lambda_2 = 3 - 2\sqrt{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Example 5.17.

    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} M=\begin{bmatrix} a \amp b\\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Express the characteristic equation of \(M\) in terms of \(\det(M)\text{.}\) What is the equation when \(M\) is singular?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that
    🔗
    \(\begin{eqnarray*} \det(M - \lambda I) \amp = \amp \begin{vmatrix} a-\lambda \amp b \\ c \amp d - \lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp = \amp (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - a\lambda - d\lambda + ad - bc \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc). \end{eqnarray*}\)
    We see that the constant term of the characteristic polynomial is \(ad - bc = \det(M)\text{.}\) Furthermore, the linear coefficient is \(-(a + d) = -\operatorname{tr}(M)\text{,}\) where \(\operatorname{tr}(M)\) is the trace of \(M\) and is the sum of its diagonal elements. When \(M\) is singular, we have \(\det(M) = 0\text{,}\) so the characteristic polynomial is \(\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda\text{,}\) which has a factor of \(\lambda\text{.}\) Therefore, we see that \(\lambda_1 = 0\) is an eigenvalue of \(M\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) are the eigenvalues of \(M\text{.}\) Then, we have that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(M)\lambda + \det(M) = (\lambda - \lambda_1)(\lambda - \lambda_2) = \lambda^2 - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)\lambda + \lambda_1\lambda_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that \(\operatorname{tr}(M)\) is the sum of the eigenvalues of \(M\) and \(\det(M)\) is the product of the eigenvalues of \(M\text{.}\) This is no coincidence and is true for all \(n\times n\) matrices.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Theorem 5.18.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n\text{.}\) Then, \(\det(A) = \lambda_1\cdots\lambda_n\) and \(\operatorname{tr}(A) = \lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Definition 5.19. Algebraic Multiplicity.

    The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue is its multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.20.

    Compute the algebraic multiplicities of the eigenvalues for
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Note that
    🔗
    \(\begin{eqnarray*}\det(A - \lambda I) \amp = \amp \begin{vmatrix} 1 - \lambda \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2-\lambda \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 - \lambda \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1-\lambda \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = \amp (1-\lambda)(2-\lambda)(-1-\lambda)(-1-\lambda) \\ \amp = \amp (1-\lambda)(2-\lambda)(-1-\lambda)^2.\end{eqnarray*}\)
    It follows that \(\lambda_1 = 1\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) with algebraic multiplicity equal to 1 and \(\lambda_2 = 2\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) with algebraic multiplicity equal to 1. Since the characteristic polynomials has a factor of \((-1-\lambda)^2\text{,}\) we see that \(\lambda_3 = -1\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) with algebraic multiplicity equal to 2.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
     
    🔗

    Definition 5.21. Geometric Multiplicity.

    The geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is the dimension of \(\operatorname{Nul}(A-\lambda I)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    1. The geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is always between 1 and the algebraic multiplicity of \(\lambda\text{.}\) It can never be 0 nor be greater than the algebraic mutliplicy.
      🔗
    2. Basic example:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
      has only eigenvalue \(\lambda=1\text{.}\) Its algebraic multiplicity is 2, but its geometric multiplicity is 1.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.22.

    Compute the geometric multiplicities of the eigenvalues for
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp 1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    As we saw in Example 5.20, the eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) (with algebraic multiplicity = 1), \(\lambda_2 = 2\) (with algebraic multiplicity = 1), and \(\lambda_3 = -1\) (with algebraic multiplicity = 2). For \(\lambda_1 = 1\text{,}\) we see that
    \begin{equation*} A - 1I = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    which has a single free variable. Thus, the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_1 = 1\) is 1 (which is actually forced since the geometric multiplicity has to be between 1 and the algebraic multiplicity which was also 1 for this eigenvalue). By similar reasoning, we see that the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_2 = 2\) is 1.
    🔗
    Now consider \(\lambda_3 = -1\text{.}\) The algebraic multiplicity of \(\lambda_3 = -1\) is 2. Therefore, the geometric multiplicity could be 1 or 2. Note that
    \begin{equation*} A - (-1)I = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    which has a single free variable. Therefore, the geometric multiplicity for \(\lambda_3 = -1\) is 1, not 2.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.23.

    For each part below, give an example of a \(5\times 5\) matrix withe the stated properties, if possible.
    🔗
    (a)
    The only eigenvalue is \(\lambda=0\text{,}\) but the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda=0\) is one.
    🔗
    Solution.
    For simplicity, we will attempt to form an upper triangular matrix with the properties. This makes things easier since we know that the eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are exactly the entries on the diagonal. Therefore, each diagonal element of our upper triangular matrix needs to be 0. This leads us to
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp \amp \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    where the blank entries are undecided. So far, we have guaranteed that every eigenvalue of \(A\) is \(\lambda = 0\text{.}\) Now, we need to force \(\lambda = 0\) to have geometric multiplicity equal to 1. This means that the matrix \(A - 0I = A\) needs to have exactly one free variable. Note that, as is, Column 1 of \(A\) already corresponds to a free variable. Thus, we need to make sure that Columns 2, 3, 4, and 5 are pivot columns. To do that, we need to put nonzero entries directly above the diagonal entry. This give the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \amp \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can fill in the remaining entries with whatever we want and \(A\) will still have the required properties.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    The only eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1=0\) and \(\lambda_2 = 1\text{.}\) The algebraic multiplicity of \(\lambda_1=0\) is three and the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_2 = 1\) is three.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can proceed in a similar manner to Task 5.23.a. Since \(\lambda_1 = 0\) has algebraic multiplicity 3, we must list it three times on the diagonal. We can fill in the remaining two entries on the diagonal with the other eigenvalue \(\lambda_2 = 1\text{.}\) This gives us
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp \amp \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that \(B\) is supposed to have the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_2 = 1\) to be 3. Unfortunately, the algebraic multiplicity of \(\lambda_2 = 1\) is 2. So it is impossible to have the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda_2 = 1\) to be 3 since the geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue can never exceed its algebraic multiplicity. It follows that creating a matrix with the desired properties is impossible. It would require at least a \(6 \times 6\) matrix.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    Exactly three eigenvalues, all with geometric multiplicity equal to 1.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that the two matrices below both satisfy the requirement and have eigenvalues \(\lambda = 1,2,3\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \quad \quad D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.24.

    Consider the Markov Chain given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \vec x_0= \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \vec x_{k+1}=P\vec x_k = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6 \amp 0.4\\ 0.4 \amp 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \vec x_k. \end{equation*}
    
    Nodes A and B with transitions 0.6 for staying and 0.4 for switching; matrix \(P=\begin{bmatrix}0.6 \amp 0.4\\ 0.4 \amp 0.6\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    In the parts below, we will use eigenvalues to describe the long-term behavior of the system.
    🔗
    (a)
    Find the eigenvalues of \(P\) and corresponding eigenvectors.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that \(\det(P) = 0.6^2 - 0.4^2 = 0.2\) and \(\operatorname{tr}(P) = 0.6 + 0.6 = 1.2\text{.}\) It follows by the result in Example 5.17 that the characteristic polynomial of \(P\) is
    \begin{equation*} \det(P-\lambda I) = \lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(P)\lambda + \det(P) = \lambda^2 -1.2\lambda + 0.2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Setting the characteristic polynomial equal to 0, multiplying both sides by 5, and factoring yields:
    \begin{equation*} 5\lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 1 = 0 \quad \implies \quad (\lambda - 1)(5\lambda - 1) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the eigenvalues of \(P\) are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = \frac{1}{5}\text{.}\) (We knew that \(\lambda = 1\) had to be an eigenvalue of \(P\) since \(P\) is a stochastic matrix.)
    🔗
    For \(\lambda_1 = 1\text{,}\) note that
    \begin{equation*} P - I = \begin{bmatrix} -0.4 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.4 \amp -0.4 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1\amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that a nonzero vector in \(\Nul(P - I)\) is \(\vec v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(P - I\) had a single free variable, we have that
    \begin{equation*} 1\text{-eigenspace}(P) = \Nul(P - I) = \Span\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For \(\lambda_2 = \frac{1}{5}\text{,}\) note that
    \begin{equation*} P - \frac{1}{5}I = P - 0.2I = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.4 \amp 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that a nonzero vector in \(\Nul(P - \frac{1}{5}I)\) is \(\vec v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(P - \frac{1}{5}I\) had a single free variable, we have that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{5}\text{-eigenspace}(P) = \Nul(P - \frac{1}{5}I) = \Span\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Use the eigenvalues and eigenvectors to determine what \(\vec x_k\) tends to as \(k\to\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that
    \begin{equation*} \vec x_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\vec v_2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are eigenvectors corresponding to \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = \frac{1}{5}\text{,}\) respectively. Note that
    \begin{equation*} \vec x_1 = P\vec x_0 = P\left(\frac{1}{2}\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\vec v_2\right) = \frac{1}{2}P\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}P\vec v_2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)\vec v_2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where we replaced \(P\vec v_1\) with \(1\vec v_1\) and replaced \(P\vec v_2\) with \(\frac{1}{5}\vec v_2\) since \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are eigenvectors of \(P\text{.}\) Similarly, we have that
    \begin{equation*} \vec x_2 = P\vec x_1 = \frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)P\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)P\vec v_2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)^2\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^2\vec v_2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and in general, \(\frac{1}{5}\vec v_2\) since \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2\) are eigenvectors of \(P\text{.}\) Similarly, we have that
    \begin{equation*} \vec x_k = P\vec x_{k-1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)^{k-1}P\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^{k-1}P\vec v_2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)^k\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^k\vec v_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, we have that
    \begin{equation*} \vec \ell = \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} \vec x_k = \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} \left(\frac{1}{2}\left(1\right)^k\vec v_1 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^k\vec v_2\right) = \frac{1}{2}\vec v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.25.

    True or False: A row replacement operation on a matrix does not change its eigenvalues.
    Solution.
    False. Note that the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda = -1, 3\) (check this), but doing the row replacement \(R_2 - 2R_1 \rightarrow R_2\) yields the matrix \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) which has eigenvalues \(\lambda = 1,-3\text{.}\)

    Example 5.26.

    For what values of \(k\) does the matrix have one real eigenvalue with algebraic multiplicity 2?
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \amp k\\ 2 \amp -6 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Note that the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is
    🔗
    \(\begin{eqnarray*}\det(A - \lambda I) \amp = \amp \begin{vmatrix} -3 - \lambda \amp k \\ 2 \amp -6-\lambda \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = \amp (-3-\lambda)(-6-\lambda) - 2k \\ \amp = \amp \lambda^2 + 9\lambda + 18 - 2k.\end{eqnarray*}\)
    Since we want \(A\) to have a single eigenvalue with algebraic multiplicity 2, we want the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) to be of the form
    \begin{equation*} (\lambda - \lambda_1)^2 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda_1 + \lambda_1^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, setting the linear coefficients of the two polynomials equal, we find
    \begin{equation*} 9 = -2\lambda_1 \quad \implies \quad \lambda_1 = -\frac{9}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Setting the constants of the two polynomials equal, we see that
    🔗
    \(\begin{eqnarray*}18 - 2k = \lambda_1^2 \amp \implies \amp 18 - 2k = \left(-\frac{9}{2}\right)^2 \\ \amp \implies \amp 18 - 2k = \frac{81}{4}\\ \amp \implies \amp -2k = \frac{9}{4}\\ \amp \implies \amp k = -\frac{9}{8}.\end{eqnarray*}\)
    Therefore, if \(k = -\frac{9}{8}\text{,}\) then \(A\) will have a single real eigenvalue with algebraic multiplicity 2.
