    Theorem 3.21. Volume Scaling by a Linear Transformation.

    If \(T_A:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n\) is the linear transformation defined by \(T_A(\vec x)=A\vec x\text{,}\) and \(S\) is any closed shape in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) then
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{volume}(T_A(S)) \;=\; \lvert \det(A)\rvert \cdot \operatorname{volume}(S). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Some observations:
    • Recall from Section 1.9 that the matrix corresponding to a shearing linear transformation has the from \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp k \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\) or \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ k \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Since each of these matrices has determinant 1, we see that shearing does not change the area of a shape.
      ðŸ”—
    • Note that \(S\) can be any closed shape. This includes parallelograms, polygons, circles, or crazy blobs, as long as the edge of the shape is connected.

    Theorem 3.23. Cramerâ€™s Rule.

    Let \(A \) be an invertible \(n \times n\) matrix and let \(\vec b \in \mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Then the system \(A\vec x = \vec b\) has the unique solution \(\vec x\) whose entires are given by
    \begin{equation*} x_i = \dfrac{\det(A_i)}{\det(A)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(A_i\) is the matrix formed by replacing the \(i^\text{th}\) column of \(A\) by \(\vec b\text{.}\)
